Thunderstorms

The amount and intensity of precipitation, flooding, or unexpected weather changes. Under some conditions, all of these elements, as well as their combinations, can produce erosion. This implies that some climates, whether due to extraordinarily severe rainfall and flooding, or lengthy droughts and high winds, offer ideal circumstances for soil erosion.

Water is one of the most powerful erosive agents, with a high ability to alter the soil surface. Beginning with little soil disruption induced by falling raindrops, splash erosion transfers tiny particles in the upper soil layer. After extended periods of heavy rain, soils lose some of their ability to absorb water if they get saturated with it. This increases surface runoff, which causes erosion.

When it rains, the soil in steep locations is carried away by the water toward the plains. Running water deposits mineral-rich soil in the riverbed, and this deposition of soil over time can affect the path of the river. This can result in floods, which can destroy both people and property. Water erosion reduces agricultural potential.
